Issues and risks

R1An undeclared major incidentCritical

TCK-1024 — Harbour Finance Group, a Gold/Enterprise account. A finance systems major outage affecting 42 users was logged as P2 and never flagged Major_Incident_Declared. It qualifies as effective P1 on two independent grounds (category and user impact), and it breached: first meaningful response took 27 minutes against a 15-minute target. Major-incident process was not invoked, so no escalation, comms, or post-incident review appear to have been triggered.

Action  Confirm whether the incident was managed informally, and correct the major-incident trigger so category and impact auto-escalate rather than relying on the logged priority.

R2Gold-tier service is failing, not healthyCritical

Gold/Enterprise accounts saw 6 of 8 tickets breach — 25% attainment, against 79% overall. Breaches hit all three Gold customers: Harbour Finance Group (2), Causeway Analytics (2), Foyle Utilities (2). Contractual exposure sits almost entirely with the accounts most likely to escalate.

Action  Treat as an account-management risk this month, not a queue-capacity trend. Assume Gold customers can reproduce these numbers themselves.

R3Priority classification is hiding the breachesHigh

7 of 43 in-scope tickets were logged below their effective priority. 4 of the 9 breaches — including the undeclared P1 — look compliant at the priority the analyst recorded and only surface once the rules are applied. Any reporting that trusts Reported_Priority will systematically understate breaches. This is the mechanism behind the draft's 96%.

Action  Automate effective-priority calculation at ticket creation instead of applying it at report time.

R4A Gold P2 with no analyst response on recordHigh

TCK-1029 — Harbour Finance Group, P2, 9 users. An auto-acknowledgement was sent, then no meaningful response was ever logged, yet the ticket shows as resolved six hours later. Counted as a breach per the rules. Either the response went unrecorded or the ticket was closed without one; both are reportable.

Action  Trace this ticket specifically. It is the single clearest instance of a recording gap that could be repeating elsewhere.

R5"2-minute average response" measures the robotMedium

40 of 43 in-scope tickets had an auto-acknowledgement as their first response. The draft's ~2-minute average is the acknowledgement bot's latency, not analyst contact. Measured on meaningful response, the median is 95 minutes and the mean 213. The automation is working; it is masking the queue rather than serving it.

Action  Retire auto-acknowledgement timings from management reporting entirely.

Breach themes

1 — Misclassification, not workload

Four breaches exist only because effective priority was applied: three P3s that were really P2s, and a P2 that was really P1. The draft attributes breaches to an analyst workload spike, but breaches are spread across the whole month (2 May – 26 May) with no mid-month cluster. The pattern points to triage rules, not capacity.

2 — BI & Reporting queue latency

4 of 9 breaches sit in BI & Reporting, all report requests. Analyst notes cite late assignment and tickets sitting unassigned overnight (TCK-1033, TCK-1046). This is the one theme the draft identifies correctly — it is real, but it is the least severe of the three.

3 — Impact-driven upgrades cluster on Gold accounts

Gold accounts breach at 75%. Two mechanisms compound: their tickets more often qualify for upgrade on user impact, and their stale CRM tier snapshots meant the upgrade was never applied at triage. Highest-tier customers received the least accurate prioritisation.

4 — Timestamp integrity

Near-universal auto-acknowledgement (40/43) plus one ticket with no meaningful response at all means first-response data cannot be taken at face value. Every reported response metric needs the auto-ack filter applied or it measures nothing.

Data quality concerns

  • 9 stale CRM tier snapshots. CRM_Tier_Snapshot disagreed with the authoritative lookup on 9 in-scope tickets. All three Gold customers were snapshotted as Standard (Causeway Analytics, Foyle Utilities), Lagan Manufacturing showed Standard instead of Silver, and TCK-1022 was blank. Directly caused the missed upgrades on TCK-1014 and TCK-1041 — two breaches.
  • Analyst notes contradict the authoritative lookup. TCK-1022 is annotated "Gold customer with 12 impacted users; effective P2", but Mourne HR Services is Standard in the lookup. It correctly remains P3 and met its target either way — no attainment impact — but it shows analysts are working from CRM tier beliefs the lookup does not support.
  • One missing meaningful-response timestamp. TCK-1029, marked resolved. Counted as a breach per the rules.
  • Ticket ordering is not chronological. TCK-1046 (26 May) appears out of sequence in the export, before TCK-1043. Cosmetic, but a sign the export is not reliably sorted.
  • No lookup gaps. Every in-scope Account_ID resolved against the tier lookup, so no CRM fallback was needed. This is the one clean dimension.

Assumptions and method

  • Exclusions (8 tickets). 2 password resets (TCK-1043, TCK-1048), 2 internal tests (TCK-1044, TCK-1049), 2 duplicates (TCK-1045, TCK-1050), 2 cancelled (TCK-1047, TCK-1051). 51 exported − 8 = 43 in scope. The draft's 50 appears to count near-everything and still lose a ticket; its arithmetic could not be reproduced.
  • Tier source. SLA_Customer_Tier from the lookup, joined on Account_ID. CRM snapshots and analyst notes were disregarded as non-authoritative.
  • Response clock. Elapsed calendar minutes from Opened_At_UTC to Meaningful_Response_At_UTC. Auto-acknowledgements excluded. No business-hours adjustment — tickets were not excluded for being raised out of hours.
  • Effective priority. Highest urgency produced by the rules; no ticket downgraded. TCK-1037 (P3 → P1, 28 users) and TCK-1010 / TCK-1032 (P3 → P2, data issues) were upgraded but met their tighter targets and are not breaches.
  • Exactly-at-threshold counts as met. Applied as specified; no in-scope ticket landed precisely on its threshold, so this affected nothing.
  • Unverifiable. Whether TCK-1024 was managed as a major incident despite the missing flag, and whether TCK-1029 received an unrecorded response. Both need a system check before this report is final.

Management summary

May SLA attainment was 79.1% (34 of 43 in-scope tickets), not the 96% in the circulated draft. There were nine breaches, not two, and three effective P1 incidents, not zero.

The most serious finding is not the attainment number. A finance systems outage affecting 42 users at our largest Gold account (TCK-1024) was logged as P2, never declared a major incident, and breached its 15-minute target. Our major-incident trigger depends on an analyst choosing the right priority, and in May it did not fire.

Gold/Enterprise accounts breached 6 of 8 tickets — 25% attainment. Stale CRM tier snapshots meant our highest-tier customers were consistently triaged as Standard. This is contractual exposure concentrated exactly where we can least afford it.

The draft's conclusion that no operational changes are required does not hold. Four of the nine breaches are invisible unless effective priority is applied, which is how 96% was reached in good faith. The reporting method, not analyst effort, is what produced the wrong answer.

Recommended to leadership: May attainment restates to 79.1% with nine breaches, including an undeclared P1 major outage at a Gold finance account. Gold-tier attainment is 25%. Three changes are required: automate effective-priority calculation at intake, refresh CRM tier snapshots against the SLA lookup, and remove auto-acknowledgement timings from SLA reporting. Recommend account-level review for Harbour Finance Group, Causeway Analytics, and Foyle Utilities before month-end.
