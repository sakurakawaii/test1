# Exhibition Style Guide
## *Echoes of Civilization: Art Across Time*
### Fairhaven Museum of Art — Interpretation & Design Department

**Version 3.2 — Approved July 8, 2026**
**Prepared by:** Interpretation & Design Department, in consultation with Curatorial, Registration, Marketing, and the Accessibility Office
**Supersedes:** Version 3.1 (May 22, 2026)
**Applies to:** All wall text, object labels, captions, donor panels, educational graphics, digital labels, gallery guides, and press materials produced for this exhibition


---

## 1. Governing Principles

1. **Clarity before elegance.** Interpretive text should be understandable to a visitor with no background in art history before it is impressive to one who has it.
2. **One idea per label.** Object labels support looking, not lecturing.
3. **Consistency across formats.** A visitor moving from a wall panel to a mobile guide to a press release should encounter the same facts, the same spellings, and the same tone.
4. **Accessibility is not a late-stage edit.** Readability and format standards (Section 6) apply from first draft, not only after accessibility review.

---

## 2. Voice and Tone

- Write in the third person. Avoid "we," "our," and "you" in object labels; "you" is permitted in family/educational panels and hands-on prompts.
- Use active voice wherever possible. ("The painter applied gold leaf directly to the panel," not "Gold leaf was applied directly to the panel by the painter.")
- Avoid connoisseurial jargon (e.g., "painterly," "chiaroscuro," "provenance" without gloss) unless the term is defined in context or in the exhibition glossary panel.
- Avoid superlatives and unverifiable claims ("the finest example," "masterpiece") unless directly attributable and sourced.
- Present tense for describing what is depicted or what the object does ("The vessel holds..."); past tense for historical events and the artist's biography.

---

## 3. Capitalization

- **Exhibition title:** Title Case, italicized in running text: *Echoes of Civilization: Art Across Time*. On graphics, the title may be set in display type without italics; italics are an editorial (running-text) convention only.
- **Gallery names:** Title Case for the full proper name (e.g., "The Blackwell Gallery of Sacred and Courtly Arts," "Origins Gallery," "Crossroads Gallery," "The Delacroix Gallery of Modern and Contemporary Echoes"). When referring to a gallery informally in body text, lowercase "gallery" ("in the Crossroads gallery") is acceptable only in internal memos, never on graphics.
- **Panel headlines:** Sentence case, not Title Case (e.g., "A world in fragments," not "A World in Fragments"). Exception: the Exhibition title itself and named galleries, per above.
- **Object titles:** Sentence case internally, italicized (e.g., *Autumn at Katsura*), except for proper nouns within the title.
- **Culture/period names:** Capitalize recognized period and cultural designations (Ming dynasty — note lowercase "dynasty" per Chicago/AAM convention; Attic; Etruscan; Moche; Akan). Do not capitalize generic terms like "ancient," "medieval," or "contemporary" unless beginning a sentence.
- **Job titles:** Capitalize only when immediately preceding a name (Chief Curator Dr. Priya Anand; Dr. Priya Anand, chief curator).

---

## 4. Punctuation

- Use curved ("smart") quotation marks throughout; straight quotes are a fabrication error and must be flagged in proofing.
- Use the em dash (—) without surrounding spaces for parenthetical breaks in running text.
- Use the en dash (–) for number ranges and date ranges (1962–1965; pp. 12–14), with no surrounding spaces.
- Oxford (serial) comma is required in all Exhibition copy.
- Avoid exclamation points except in family/educational panel prompts, and no more than one per panel.
- Do not use ampersands in running text; ampersands are permitted only in donor and institutional names that use them as part of the legal or preferred name (e.g., a firm's own use of "&").

---

## 5. Typography and Layout

- **Panel hierarchy on graphics:**
  - Level 1 — Exhibition title (display)
  - Level 2 — Gallery title
  - Level 3 — Section/thematic panel headline
  - Level 4 — Object label headline (artist or object name)
  - Body copy
  - Credit line (smallest size, distinct from body copy)
- **Typeface:** Sans-serif for all identifying data (artist, title, date, medium, dimensions, credit line, accession number); serif or sans-serif permitted for extended interpretive body copy, consistent within a single panel type.
- **Minimum body text size:** 18-point equivalent at intended viewing distance for primary object labels; 24-point minimum for section panels; 48-point minimum for the Exhibition title treatment. (See Section 6 for accessibility minimums, which govern where they are more restrictive.)
- **Line length:** 40–70 characters per line for body copy.
- **Alignment:** Left-aligned, ragged right. Justified text is not permitted on any Exhibition graphic.
- **Case:** Avoid setting body copy in all capitals. All caps is permitted only for short wayfinding labels (e.g., "EXIT," gallery numbers) and acronyms.

---

## 6. Accessibility Standards

These standards are mandatory minimums, informed by WCAG 2.1 AA guidance as adapted for physical exhibition graphics, and by the Fairhaven Museum of Art ADA Compliance Checklist.

- **Contrast ratio:** Minimum 4.5:1 between text and background for body copy; minimum 3:1 for large display type (24pt+ bold or 18pt+ regular equivalent).
- **Text size:** Primary labels no smaller than 18pt equivalent; extended labels and section panels no smaller than 24pt equivalent, measured at the standard 24-inch reading distance used for rail-mounted labels.
- **Mounting height:** Label centerline at 46–48 inches from finished floor; label top edge no higher than 60 inches to remain within reach and sightline range for seated visitors.
- **Reading level:** Primary object labels should target a Flesch-Kincaid grade level of 7–8. Section and gallery panels may run to grade 9 where subject matter requires it, with justification noted in the accessibility review file.
- **Word counts:**
  - Object labels: 75–125 words (interpretive text only; identifying data is separate and not counted toward this limit)
  - Section/thematic panels: 150–200 words
  - Gallery introduction panels: 200–275 words
  - Exhibition introductory wall panel: 275–325 words
- **Alt text and audio guide parity:** Every panel with visual-only content (maps, diagrams, timelines) requires equivalent audio-guide or large-print description. Audio guide scripts should closely track panel text but may expand up to 25% to accommodate spoken cadence.
- **Large-print and multilingual formats:** A large-print binder (18pt minimum, high-contrast) must be produced for every object label and panel. Spanish-language translations are provided for the Exhibition title, all gallery titles, all section panels, and the Exhibition introductory wall panel; object-level labels are not translated in full but include a one-line Spanish summary where wall space allows.
- **Tactile and multisensory access:** At least one object per gallery should be identified as a candidate for a tactile reproduction or touchable material sample, coordinated with the Accessibility Coordinator and Registrar.

## 7. Object Label Structure and Order

Standard order for all individual object labels, gallery labels, and object captions:

1. **Artist name** (or "Artist unknown" / culture-of-origin attribution), with nationality and life dates in parentheses: *Surname, Firstname (Nationality, birth–death)*. For anonymous or workshop attributions, use the culture or attribution format shown in Section 8.
2. **Title**, italicized, sentence case.
3. **Date**, formatted per Section 9.
4. **Medium**, lowercase, comma-separated list of materials.
5. **Dimensions**, formatted per Section 10.
6. **Credit line**, formatted per Section 11.
7. **Accession number**, formatted as YYYY.NN.N (year of accession, sequential lot, sequential item).
8. **Interpretive text**, 75–125 words, one paragraph unless the object requires a discrete provenance or content note (see Section 8 for sensitive material).

Identifying data (lines 1–7) is set as a single compact block, left-aligned, in the label's smallest text size but never below the 18pt accessibility minimum. Interpretive text follows as a separate paragraph block.

---

## 8. Attribution and Sensitive-Content Conventions

- **Unknown maker:** Use "Artist unknown" rather than "Anonymous."
- **Culture-based attribution (works not attributed to a single named maker):** Lead with the culture or workshop, not "Artist unknown," when the object is customarily catalogued by culture of origin (e.g., "Court of Benin (present-day Nigeria)," "Akan peoples, Ghana," "Moche, north coast Peru"). Do not use outdated ethnonyms or colonial-era place names; use current nation-state names in parentheses where the historical polity does not map to a modern equivalent.
- **Workshop and circle attributions:** Use "Attributed to the Circle of [Name]," "Workshop of [Name]," or "Follower of [Name]" per standard connoisseurship terminology; do not shorten to the artist's name alone.
- **Objects with contested acquisition histories:** Any object with an incomplete, disputed, or actively-researched provenance record (flagged by Registration) requires a provenance note, reviewed jointly by Curatorial and Registration before fabrication. Language must be factual and non-speculative; do not characterize ongoing research as resolved. See the Registrar's provenance addendum for object-specific approved language.
- **Human remains and sacred/ceremonial objects:** None are included in this Exhibition. Any future addition requires Collections Committee review prior to interpretive drafting.

---

## 9. Date Formatting

- Use **BCE/CE**, not BC/AD, throughout.
- Use "**ca.**" (not "c." or "circa," spelled out) for approximate dates: *ca. 530 BCE*.
- Date ranges use an en dash with no surrounding spaces: *1522–1566*.
- Centuries are spelled out and lowercase: *17th century* (not "17th Century" or "seventeenth century" in label copy; the spelled-out form is reserved for extended essay text such as catalogue entries, not object labels).
- Dynastic or reign-based dates should include a CE/BCE equivalent range in parentheses on first use within a label: *Ming dynasty, Jiajing period (1522–1566)*.
- Artist life dates use an en dash, no spaces: *(1868–1943)*. Living artists: birth year followed by "b." — *(b. 1985)*.

---

## 10. Measurements

- Format: height precedes width precedes depth, abbreviated **H. x W. x D.** Two-dimensional objects (paintings, works on paper) list height by width only, and the H./W. abbreviations may be dropped when unambiguous (e.g., a framed painting label may read simply "19 3/4 x 26 in.").
- Primary unit is **inches**, with centimeters in parentheses: *H. 16 1/8 in. (41 cm)*.
- Use fractions (1/8, 1/4, 1/2, etc.), not decimals, for inch measurements on object labels; decimals are acceptable in the registrar's internal object records but must be converted for public-facing copy.
- Diameter is abbreviated **Diam.**
- For irregular or installation-variable works, use "Dimensions variable" and note approximate footprint in a registrar's internal note (not on the public label).

---

## 11. Credit Lines

- Set in a distinct, smaller text size from interpretive copy, directly beneath the accession number.
- Standard forms, used exactly as follows:
  - *Gift of [Name(s)]*
  - *Museum purchase*
  - *Museum purchase with funds provided by [Name/Fund]*
  - *Bequest of [Name]*
  - *Gift of the Estate of [Name]*
  - *Gift of the artist*
  - *Commissioned for [Exhibition title] with support from [Donor/Fund]*
- Do not use a terminal period after a credit line unless it ends in an abbreviation.
- Donor names in credit lines follow the donor's own preferred form, confirmed against the Development Office's donor record, not assumed from prior publications. Couples are credited as recorded by Development (e.g., "Dr. and Mrs. Harold Fenwick" or "Owen and Harriet Blackwell" — the Museum uses the form each donor has specified and does not standardize across donors).

## Appendix K — Interim Exceptions and Legacy Rules

This appendix was added after Version 3.2 and was not incorporated into the numbered rules above. Where this appendix conflicts with the main guide, departments were instructed to use the rule most recently approved for their own use case. No central record defines which approval date controls when two departments approved different variants.

Legacy labels may use BCE/CE without periods, while newly written labels must use B.C.E./C.E. with periods. Communications may omit accession numbers online, but press captions should retain them when supplied by Registration. Fabrication may preserve already-typeset punctuation even when it conflicts with copyediting standards, provided no factual meaning changes. Accessibility may replace semicolons with periods without triggering a new curatorial review.

“Final,” “approved,” “released,” and “cleared” are workflow terms and are not necessarily equivalent. A file name containing FINAL is not evidence of final status; however, the shared-drive manifest may be used as a practical tie-breaker when no newer signed record exists.



## Embedded Department Record 1 — Circulating Extract

Revision 4.1 | Issued May 12, 2026
Owner: Office of Exhibitions Documentation

This protocol governs how exhibition information moves from internal records into public products. It does not itself approve any exhibition claim.

A claim is publishable only when the content owner has approved the underlying fact and the channel owner has approved that specific use. Fabrication release permits physical production from the released file but does not authorize reuse on the website, in press materials, or in fundraising copy. Press approval permits use only after any listed embargo and only with the approved image or caption. Wayfinding approval covers orientation language, not interpretive claims.

When records conflict, use the decision that is both later in the decision sequence and issued by the responsible owner. Sequence is established by referenced meeting action numbers, approval initials, or explicit incorporation into a release packet. File modification dates and labels such as “final,” “clean,” or “current” are not authority by themselves.

A change notice replaces only the fields or passages it names. Everything else in the earlier approved record remains in force. Meeting notes become controlling only where an assigned owner records an accepted action and the later approval tracker marks that action complete. Email statements may clarify scope but do not replace an approval unless the responsible owner explicitly ratifies the change.

Required ownership:
- Curatorial: title, gallery structure, interpretation, object selection, dating, attribution.
- Registration: accession data, dimensions, credit lines, provenance status, loan status.
- Development: donor names, tiers, and recognition wording.
- Accessibility: statements about available access features and completion status.
- Communications: web and press channel approval.
- Rights and Reproductions: image use and embargo conditions.
- Visitor Experience: public hours, opening access, and wayfinding.

If a source is missing, do not treat silence as approval. If two sources repeat the same claim from a common draft, they count as one chain of evidence, not independent confirmation.
