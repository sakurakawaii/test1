Reference summary — DNA damage and degradation in herbarium plant tissue
(Condensed from: Staats M, Cuenca A, Richardson JE, Vrielink-van Ginkel R, Petersen G, Seberg O, Bakker FT. DNA Damage in Plant Herbarium Tissue. PLOS ONE, 2011, 6(12), e28448. DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0028448.)

Herbarium specimens are a valuable but challenging source of DNA for genetic studies, since the drying and preservation process typically leaves DNA highly fragmented. The authors compared fresh leaf tissue against herbarium specimens of the same individual plants — both freshly-dried ("young" herbarium material) and long-stored specimens up to 114 years old ("old" herbarium material) — across four species.

Fresh tissue showed high molecular weight DNA with no significant fragmentation. Young herbarium material, despite being processed only hours earlier, already showed clear signs of degradation, with average fragment sizes dropping below roughly 10kb. Old herbarium specimens were far more degraded, with fragment sizes mostly falling below 1kb.

The authors found that the majority of DNA damage occurs during the initial drying/preparation process itself, rather than accumulating gradually during long-term storage — meaning a freshly-dried specimen and a decades-old specimen may not differ as dramatically as expected once past that initial preparation stage. They also found no evidence that any particular part of the genome (plastid, mitochondrial, or nuclear) degrades faster than another during this process, though older specimens did show a specific type of chemical DNA damage (cytosine deamination) that can introduce sequencing errors if not accounted for.

Practically, the authors note that a smeared, low-molecular-weight gel pattern is the expected, normal appearance for degraded herbarium DNA, not necessarily a sign of a failed extraction.
