## *Echoes of Civilization: Art Across Time*

## Panel 1: What Does "Echo" Mean in Art?
**Location:** Entrance to Origins Gallery
**Word count:** 92

Say something in a canyon, and it comes back to you a moment later, a little changed. That's an echo.

Art can echo too. An artist working today might borrow a shape, a color, or an idea from something made thousands of years ago — not by copying it exactly, but by answering it in a new way.

As you walk through this exhibition, look for the word ECHO on the floor. It marks two objects, far apart in time, that are somehow in conversation. Can you guess what they have in common before you read the label?

## Panel 2: Try This — Spot the Echo
**Location:** Crossroads Gallery, interactive rail
**Word count:** 88

**Look at the goldweight in this case.** It's smaller than your thumb, but it once helped decide how much gold was worth in trade.

**Now find *Ancestral Cloth (Kente Study)* in the next gallery.** The artist, Amara Osei, wove patterns that carry meaning the same way the goldweight's shape did.

**Ask yourself:**
- What is the smallest object you own that carries a big meaning?
- If you designed a symbol to represent your family, what would it look like?

Turn the corner to see how one more artist answered this question.

## Panel 3: How Do Museums Care for Ancient Objects?
**Location:** Origins Gallery, case-adjacent rail
**Word count:** 105

The shabti figure in this case is over three thousand years old and made of a delicate material called faience. So how has it survived this long?

Museums control temperature, humidity, and light to slow down the natural aging of objects. Conservators — specialists trained in caring for art and artifacts — examine objects regularly for cracks, fading, or damage, and make careful repairs when needed, often using techniques that can be reversed if better methods are developed later.

Every object in this exhibition traveled here in a padded crate, was unpacked by trained handlers wearing gloves, and was placed in its case using tools designed to avoid any direct contact with bare hands.

## Panel 4: Meet the Conservator
**Location:** Blackwell Gallery, rail panel
**Word count:** 97

Before the Book of Hours leaf in this case went on view, it spent three weeks in the Museum's conservation lab.

Paper and parchment conservator Ruth Okafor examined the leaf under magnification, checking the gold leaf for lifting edges and the vellum for signs of past water damage. She stabilized a small tear along the bottom margin using wheat-starch paste, a material conservators favor because it can be safely undone by a future conservator if needed.

"We're not trying to make it look new," Okafor says. "We're trying to make sure it survives long enough for someone else to study it."

## Panel 5: Materials Across Time
**Location:** Delacroix Gallery, exit rail
**Word count:** 110

Look back at the materials you've seen today: clay, bronze, gold leaf, glass, brass, silk, marble.

Every one of those materials still shows up in the final gallery, in the hands of artists working right now. Naledi Mokoena still shapes clay by hand. Marcus Ohene still casts bronze. Amara Osei still weaves silk on a loom not so different from ones used centuries ago.

Materials don't expire. A technique invented a thousand years ago can still be the right tool for an idea today.

**Before you leave:** Which material from this exhibition would you want to work with, and what would you make?
