## GALLERY 1 — ORIGINS GALLERY

### 1.1
**Artist unknown (Egyptian, New Kingdom, Dynasty 19)**
*Shabti figure*
ca. 1250 BCE
Faience
H. 4 3/4 in. (12 cm)
Gift of Dr. and Mrs. Harold Fenwick
1955.2.8

Shabti figures were placed in tombs to perform labor on behalf of the deceased in the afterlife. Inscribed with a spell from the Book of the Dead, this figure would "answer" when its owner was called to work, freeing them for rest. Small enough to hold in one hand, it reflects an economy of belief in which the smallest object could carry the largest promise: a life that continues.

### 1.2
**Attributed to the Circle of the Antimenes Painter (Greek, Attic)**
*Amphora with departing warrior*
ca. 530 BCE
Terracotta, black-figure decoration
H. 16 1/8 in. (41 cm)
Museum purchase with funds provided by the Ainsworth Acquisition Fund
1987.14.2

A young warrior takes leave of his family, spear in hand, as an older man extends a farewell cup. Scenes like this decorated vessels used at banquets, where the boundary between daily life and myth was thin. The amphora's shape and story would have been read together by its first owners, the painted narrative unfolding as the vessel was turned in the hand.

### 1.3
**Etruscan**
*Mirror with engraved back*
ca. 350 BCE
Bronze
Diam. 6 1/2 in. (16.5 cm)
Gift of Robert and Elaine Sung
1968.4.3

The reverse of this mirror is engraved with a mythological scene, while the polished front once reflected its owner's face. Bronze mirrors were common possessions among Etruscan women and were often placed in tombs, suggesting that self-reflection — literal and otherwise — was considered a fitting companion for the afterlife.

### 1.4
**Roman**
*Torso of a youth*
1st century CE
Marble
H. 27 in. (68.5 cm)
Museum purchase
1962.8.1

This fragment likely once belonged to a full-length statue, possibly a copy after an earlier Greek original. The Romans prized Greek sculpture and reproduced it widely for private villas and public spaces. What survives is not the whole figure but its posture: weight shifted onto one leg, the beginning of a turn we can no longer follow to completion.

### 1.5
**Roman**
*Unguentarium*
2nd–3rd century CE
Blown glass
H. 4 in. (10.2 cm)
Gift of the Estate of Marion Cole Whitfield
1979.15.2

This small glass vessel held perfumed oil, likely used in funerary rites or daily grooming. Glassblowing, developed in the 1st century BCE, transformed glass from a luxury reserved for the wealthy into an everyday material. The vessel's slightly uneven walls are evidence of the human breath that shaped it.

### 1.6
**Greek**
*Grave stele of a young woman*
ca. 350 BCE
Marble
H. 41 in. (104 cm)
Museum purchase
1958.1.4

The young woman shown here holds a small box, likely containing jewelry, while a servant looks on. Grave stelae like this one marked burial sites along roads leading out of Greek cities, ensuring that the dead remained part of the visual life of the living. The scene is quiet, domestic, and deliberately unremarkable — grief rendered as routine.

## GALLERY 2 — THE BLACKWELL GALLERY OF SACRED AND COURTLY ARTS

### 2.1
**Artist unknown (French, Paris)**
*Leaf from a Book of Hours: The Annunciation*
ca. 1400
Tempera, ink, and gold on vellum
7 3/8 x 5 1/4 in. (18.7 x 13.3 cm)
Gift of Owen and Harriet Blackwell
1991.11.3

Books of Hours guided private prayer through the canonical hours of the day. This leaf illustrates the Annunciation, the moment the angel Gabriel tells Mary she will bear a son. Gold leaf catches candlelight, turning a small handheld object into something that glows in low light — devotion designed for dim rooms and quiet reading.

### 2.2
**Limoges, France**
*Reliquary casket*
ca. 1200
Gilt copper and champlevé enamel on wood core
H. 5 1/2 x W. 6 3/4 x D. 3 in. (14 x 17.1 x 7.6 cm)
Gift of Owen and Harriet Blackwell
1973.9.1

Reliquaries housed the physical remains or possessions of saints, believed to carry spiritual power. Limoges workshops produced enamel objects like this one for churches and private chapels across medieval Europe, using a technique — champlevé — in which powdered glass is fired into carved recesses in copper. The casket's small scale belies the outsized devotion it was built to contain.

### 2.3
**Court of Benin (present-day Nigeria)**
*Plaque with a warrior chief and attendants*
17th century
Brass
H. 18 1/2 x W. 13 in. (47 x 33 cm)
Gift of the Estate of Marion Cole Whitfield
1975.3.9

This plaque once adorned the royal palace at Benin City, part of a tradition of brass-casting reserved for the Oba's court. The warrior chief is shown larger than his attendants, a visual convention indicating rank rather than physical stature.

*A note on this object's history:* Many Benin court objects, including plaques of this type, left Nigeria following the British punitive expedition of 1897 and entered private and museum collections through a range of subsequent transactions. This plaque's documented history at the Museum begins with its 1975 gift from the estate of Marion Cole Whitfield; earlier ownership prior to that date is not fully documented. The Museum's Registration department is engaged in ongoing provenance research on Benin court objects in the collection, consistent with international guidance on works with this acquisition history, and welcomes inquiries.

### 2.4
**Iran (Kashan)**
*Bowl with luster decoration*
13th century
Fritware with luster glaze
Diam. 8 in. (20.3 cm)
Museum purchase
1966.12.7

Luster glaze, achieved through a demanding second firing in a reduced-oxygen kiln, produces a metallic sheen across the surface of the bowl. The technique, developed in the Islamic world, traveled along trade routes into Europe over the following centuries, where it influenced ceramic production as far away as Spain and Italy.

## GALLERY 3 — CROSSROADS GALLERY

### 3.1
**China, Ming dynasty, Jiajing period (1522–1566)**
*Vase with dragon motif*
Porcelain with underglaze cobalt blue
H. 14 1/4 in. (36.2 cm)
Gift of Dr. and Mrs. Harold Fenwick
1978.6.14

Cobalt for blue-and-white porcelain was often imported from Persia before being painted and fired in Chinese kilns, making even a single vase the product of a trade network spanning thousand of miles. The five-clawed dragon marks this as a design associated with imperial authority.

### 3.2
**Moche, north coast Peru**
*Stirrup-spout vessel*
ca. 400–600 CE
Ceramic with slip decoration
H. 9 in. (22.9 cm)
Museum purchase
1981.2.11

The stirrup-shaped spout is a hallmark of Moche ceramic production, found on vessels ranging from portrait heads to depictions of animals and deities. This example's painted decoration likely once depicted a ceremonial scene; much of the surface has worn away over the centuries it spent in a coastal tomb.

### 3.3
**Akan peoples, Ghana**
*Goldweight in the form of a bird*
19th century
Cast brass
H. 2 in. (5.1 cm)
Gift of Robert and Elaine Sung
1990.7.3

Goldweights like this one were used to measure gold dust, the primary currency across much of West Africa before colonial-era coinage. Cast in a wide range of figurative and geometric forms, they doubled as teaching tools, each shape tied to a proverb passed down through generations of traders.

### 3.4
**Workshop of Pieter Claesz (Dutch, active Haarlem)**
*Vanitas still life*
ca. 1650
Oil on panel
19 3/4 x 26 in. (50.2 x 66 cm)
Bequest of Marion Cole Whitfield
1969.3.8

A skull, an extinguished candle, and an overturned glass remind the viewer that earthly pleasure is fleeting. Vanitas paintings flourished in the Dutch Republic during a period of expanding global trade, when porcelain, spices, and tobacco — some of it visible in paintings like this one — flowed into Europe through networks connecting Asia, the Americas, and Africa.

### 3.5
**Kōga Michinari (Japanese, 1868–1943)**
*Autumn at Katsura*
1921
Hanging scroll; ink and color on silk
52 x 21 1/2 in. (132 x 54.6 cm)
Gift of the Ainsworth Charitable Trust
2004.22.6

Painted during a period when Japanese artists were renegotiating centuries-old painting traditions against new influences from Europe, this scroll depicts the Katsura River south of Kyoto in early autumn. Kōga trained in the Kanō school before developing a looser, more atmospheric style, evident in the soft transitions between ink and color here.

## GALLERY 4 — THE DELACROIX GALLERY OF MODERN AND CONTEMPORARY ECHOES

### 4.1
**Naledi Mokoena (South African, b. 1985)**
*Vessel Memory I*
2022
Ceramic, gold leaf, and graphite
H. 22 x Diam. 14 in. (55.9 x 35.6 cm)
Commissioned for *Echoes of Civilization: Art Across Time* with support from the Harlan Family Foundation
2023.5.1

Mokoena built this vessel by hand, then deliberately cracked and repaired it using gold leaf along the fracture lines — a technique she developed independently, distinct from but often discussed alongside Japanese *kintsugi*. She created the work in direct response to Gallery 1's funerary objects, asking what it means for a vessel to hold not liquid but time itself.

### 4.2
**Marcus Ohene (British-Ghanaian, b. 1990)**
*Reliquary (after Benin)*
2021
Bronze, brass, and reclaimed wood
H. 24 x W. 16 x D. 9 in. (61 x 40.6 x 22.9 cm)
Museum purchase with funds provided by the Contemporary Collectors Circle
2022.14.2

Ohene's sculpture reworks the form of a Benin court plaque into an open, unfinished structure, its brass elements cast from molds of his own hands. Made in direct dialogue with the plaque on view in Gallery 2, the work asks what remains of an object once it is separated from the palace, the ceremony, and the community it was made to serve.

### 4.3
**Elena Vasquez-Torres (American, b. 1978)**
*Palimpsest No. 4*
2020
Oil and gold leaf on panel
48 x 36 in. (121.9 x 91.4 cm)
Gift of the artist
2021.9.5

Layers of paint are scraped back and repainted across this panel, revealing earlier compositions beneath the final image. Vasquez-Torres has described her process as a way of making time visible on a flat surface, echoing the way illuminated manuscripts, like the Book of Hours leaf in Gallery 2, were sometimes reused and overwritten.

### 4.4
**Dana Whitfield-Marsh (American, b. 1972)**
*Echo Chamber*
2019
Cast bronze and steel
H. 68 x W. 30 x D. 30 in. (172.7 x 76.2 x 76.2 cm)
Museum purchase
2020.6.1

This freestanding sculpture takes the form of an oversized, hollow vessel pierced with irregular openings. Visitors are invited to walk around, though not inside, the work. Whitfield-Marsh has cited ancient Greek and Etruscan funerary containers as a starting point, though the sculpture's rough, unfinished bronze surface resists the polish typically associated with commemorative objects.

### 4.5
**Amara Osei (Ghanaian American, b. 1988)**
*Ancestral Cloth (Kente Study)*
2021
Handwoven cotton and silk
84 x 52 in. (213.4 x 132.1 cm)
Gift of the artist and Meridian Trust Bank
2022.3.9

Osei apprenticed with weavers in Bonwire, Ghana, before developing this large-scale reinterpretation of traditional kente cloth patterns. Each strip carries a modified version of a named pattern, altered to reflect Osei's own family history. The work stands in visual dialogue with the goldweights in Gallery 3, both objects built from systems of pattern that carry embedded meaning.

### 4.6
**Ingrid Solberg (Norwegian, b. 1965)**
*Ruins Suite No. 7*
2018
Archival pigment print
30 x 40 in. (76.2 x 101.6 cm)
Museum purchase
2019.11.2

Solberg photographs excavation sites at the moment archaeologists pause their work, capturing half-uncovered walls and tools left mid-task. This image, taken at a Roman-era site, deliberately withholds the "finished" view of an ancient ruin, presenting instead an interrupted process that never fully resolves into a picture of the past.

### 4.7
**Julian Reyes (American, b. 1980)**
*Stratum*
2022
Mixed media on canvas
60 x 84 in. (152.4 x 213.4 cm)
Gift of the Harlan Family Foundation
2023.1.7

Built up from sand, pigment, and fragments of ceramic shard, Reyes's canvas reads as a cross-section of earth, layered with time. The work closes the exhibition's fourth gallery, gathering references to nearly every material seen in the galleries before it — clay, glaze, metal, stone — into a single surface.
